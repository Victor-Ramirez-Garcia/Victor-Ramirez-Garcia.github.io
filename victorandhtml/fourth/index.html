Examples of folks exercising their Fourth Amendment Right

Michigan Department of the State Police v. Sitz
(Feb 27, 1990 - Jun 14, 1990):

Brief Summary(Scene happened in 1986):

Rick Sitz, a licensed Michigan driver, challenged sobriety checkpoints that the Michigan State Police Department created for a program thats aimed to reduce drunk driving within the state.

Court Decision:

Rick Sitz was successfull in the Michigan lower courts, but in a 6-to-3 majority in the Rehnquist Court, the Court held that the roadblocks did not violate the Fourth Amendment.

"Michigan Department of State Police v. Sitz." Oyez, www.oyez.org/cases/1989/88-1897. Accessed 27 Sep. 2021.

Adams v. Williams
(Apr 10, 1972 - Jun 12, 1972):

Brief Summary(Scene happened during the early morning hours of October 30, 1966):

An indiviadual approached police officer Frederick E. Adams and informed him that an indivual in a nearby vehicle was carrying narcotics and had a gun at his waist. Adams then approached the vehicle, reached into the car, and removed a gun from Williams waistband once he got told to rolled down the window. Police Officer Frederick E. Adams then arrested Robert Williams for unlawful possession of a firearm and proceeded to search the vehicle, where he found heroin. The Supreme Court of Connecticut affirmed the conviction of Williams for possession of a handgun and heroin, which Williams later then filed a claim against Adams that the state of Connecticut continued to detain him unlawfully as a prisoner through an unlawful search and should not use the handgun and drugs into evidence at his trial.

Court Decision:

Robert Williams was then ordered to let his conviction be set aside by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit. On the contrary, in a 6-3 majority in the Burger Court, the Court held that the Fourth Amendment allows a police officer, acting only on a tip of an informant, to approach a person and remove a weapon concealed in the person's waistband.

"Adams v. Williams." Oyez, www.oyez.org/cases/1971/70-283. Accessed 27 Sep. 2021.

Examples of The Fourth Amendment Right being violated

Katz v. The United States
(Oct 17, 1967 - Dec 18, 1967):

Summary:

Federal Agents attached an eavesdropping device to the outside of a public phone booth used by Katz on a hunch that Katz was transmitting gambling information over the phone to clients in other states. After hearing the recording of the conversations, Katz was convicted under an eight-count indictment for the illegal transmission of wagering information from Los Angeles to Boston and Miami. Kats then challenged stating that the recordings could not be used as evidence against him which the Court of Appeals denied his appeal, but granted certiorari, an order by which a higher court reviews a decision of a lower court.

Court Decision:

In a 7-to-1 majority in the Warren Court, the Court held that the United States violated Katz Fourth Amendment by the unnecessary physical intrusion into the area he occupied and the protection of Katz conversations.

"Katz v. United States." Oyez, www.oyez.org/cases/1967/35. Accessed 27 Sep. 2021.

City of Indianapolis v. Edmond
(Oct 3, 2000 - Nov 28, 2000):

Brief Summary(Scene happened in 1998):

Both James Edmond and Joell Palmer were stopped at one of the City of Indianapolis narcotics checkpoints that conducts an open-view examination of a vehicle without reasonable suspicion or probable cause of unlawful drugs. Both James and Joell filed a lawsuit at the city of Indianapolis on their behalf and the class of motorists who had been stopped or were subject to being stopped, alleging that the roadblocks violated the Fourth Amendment.

Court Decision:

The District Court rejected the request for a preliminary injuction, but in a 6-3 majority in the Rehnquist Court, the Court held that because the checkpoint programs primary purpose was indistinguishable from the general interest of crime control, the checkpoints violated the Fourth Amendment.

"City of Indianapolis v. Edmond." Oyez, www.oyez.org/cases/2000/99-1030. Accessed 27 Sep. 2021.

An interesting case related to the Fourth Amendment

Arizona v. Johnson
(Dec 9, 2008 - Jan 26, 2009):

Summary:

Lemon Johnson was riding in the backseat of a car when it was pulled over by the state police in Sugar Hill, Arizona because the license of the car had a "mandatory insurance suspension". The police started to question about the car’s occupants and Johnson about gang activity in the area even though the stop was only for the suspended license. From there, Johnson voluntarily exited the car to be question further by the police. When Johnson exited the car, the police then did a subsequent search were they found a handgun and a small amount of marijuana. Johnson was then convicted in Arizona State court for unlawful possession of a weapon as a prohibited possessor and possession of marijuana. Johnson then appealed arguing that the evidence should not be used because the officers did not have probable cause to search him at the time of his arrest which is a violation of his rights under the Fourth Amendment.

Court Decision:

The Court of Appeals of Arizona granted to reversed his conviction and his sentence because the officers has no reason to believe that Johnson was involved in any criminal activity when he was searched, therefore the officers subsequent search of Johnson was illegal and unconstitutional. On reverse, in a unanimous majority in the Roberts Court, the police may lawfully search an individual during a routine traffic stop if they believe him to be armed and dangerous, even if they had no reason to believe he was involved in criminal activity when he was searched.

"Arizona v. Johnson." Oyez, www.oyez.org/cases/2008/07-1122. Accessed 27 Sep. 2021.
